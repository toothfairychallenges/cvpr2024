Call for papers

TL;DR The workshop focuses on the challenges and techniques for segmenting anatomical structures in large 3D medical volumes. It aims to facilitate collaboration among researchers, practitioners, and industry professionals to address the complexities and technical subtleties involved in such a critical domain.

The number of CT examinations conducted and the availability of computational capabilities have steadily increased in the recent years [1, 2, 3]. Furthermore, advancements in deep learning techniques have substantially enhanced the proficiency of image analysis algorithms [4, 5, 6]. As a result of the increase in computational power, data availability, and algorithm quality, recent medical studies are usually based on larger samples obtained by increasingly powerful radiological machines and scanners. In many of these studies, a precise segmentation of anatomical structures is useful for extracting advanced biomarkers, automatically detecting abnormalities, quantifying tumor load, or measuring distances between anatomical structures [7, 8].

Segmentation is currently employed in various applications, including the planning of surgical interventions, with the aim of preserving noble anatomical structure [9], and radiation therapy procedures [10]. Segmentation algorithms have the potential to be incorporated into regular clinical practice, thereby enhancing the quality of radiologic reports and mitigating the workload imposed on radiologists.

As a matter of fact, for the majority of medical applications, the initial phase involves the segmentation of pertinent anatomical structures. However, the construction and training of segmentation algorithms present intricate challenges, necessitating meticulous manual annotation of training data and technical proficiency for algorithmic training. As mentioned, one of the main challenges is represented by the dimensionality of the modern input data, which hardly fits the current hardware capabilities, requiring for distributed training or patch-based learning.

For such reasons, learning using patch-based representations has become increasingly popular in recent years: there are specific scenarios that make patch-based learning the only viable approach, e.g., when targeting complex, high-dimensional inputs, or when the computation resources available are limited or should be kept so. The segmentation or classification in whole-slide images, as well as the segmentation of anatomical structures in 3D volumes, are noticeable medical imaging applications requiring such a kind of learning procedure. Indeed, feeding a neural network with gigapixel images or hundreds of millions of voxels coming from 3D volumes is not a feasible approach.

To overcome this limitation, it is common in medical imaging to train neural networks using subsets extracted from the original data [11, 12, 13]. Such an approach mitigates memory constraints, but it also introduces additional hurdles that must be taken into account: the loss of global information due to restricted, patch-limited receptive fields, ambiguity in segmenting objects situated at the intersections of multiple patches, and potential artifacts arising from the boundaries of these patches. Furthermore, these challenges become particularly prominent when the object to be segmented is small in comparison to the entire volume: the segmentation of the IAC is an example of such circumstances [12].

Our workshop aims to delve into the advanced techniques and challenges in segmenting anatomical structures in large data, with a specific emphasis on large 3D volumes obtained with (Cone Beam) Computed Tomography. The workshop will provide a platform for researchers, practitioners, and industry professionals to discuss novel approaches, methodologies, and applications in this critical domain. We aim to foster collaboration and innovation to address the unique complexities associated with large volume segmentation.

This workshop aims at collecting novel proposals about segmentation performed in large 3D medical volumes, as (CB)CTs.

Topics of interest for LaMDaS include, but are not limited to:

  • Methodologies that tackle the memory footprint or the computational complexity of training large deep learning models on large medical datasets;
  • Architectures that improve the current state of the art in the segmentation of one or multiple anatomical structures;
  • Specific approach that aims at improving the segmentation performance of already existing deep learning models, without the requirement of training from scratch a neural network;
  • Foundational models for the segmentation of medical data, including 3D volumes.
